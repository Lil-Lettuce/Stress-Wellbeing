Background

Stress is a common issue among students and can affect academic performance, mental health, and physical wellbeing. This project focuses on understanding stress and identifying practical ways students can reduce and manage it.

What Is Stress?

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), stress is a state of worry or mental tension caused by challenging situations (WHO, 2023).

Stress is not always harmful. Eustress can be motivating, while distress occurs when stress becomes overwhelming or constant (APA, 2022).

Why Stress Should Be Addressed

  • Can negatively affect physical health (APA, 2022)
  • Can reduce concentration and memory (APA, 2022)
  • May impact long-term mental wellbeing (WHO, 2023)

Prolonged stress can reduce concentration, impair memory, and lower academic confidence. Over time, this can lead to burnout and reduced motivation, affecting both academic performance and overall wellbeing.

The Bathtub Model

Stress can be compared to water in a bathtub. Stress levels increase when inflow is high and decrease when outflow is increased.

  • Reducing inflow focuses on preventing stress
  • Increasing outflow focuses on relieving stress

This model explains why stress can quickly become overwhelming for students. When factors such as academic workload, lack of sleep, and exam pressure increase at the same time, stress accumulates rapidly. Without effective coping strategies, stress is not released, leading to distress.

Reducing Stress Inflow (Prevention)

Sleep

Poor sleep increases stress hormones such as cortisol. Maintaining a consistent sleep routine can help reduce stress.

According to our survey, approximately 30% of students reported sleeping fewer than five hours per night. Insufficient sleep affects emotional regulation and concentration, making students more vulnerable to stress, particularly during periods of high academic demand such as examinations.

Managing Workload

Procrastination and poor planning can lead to overwhelming stress. Managing workload consistently helps reduce academic pressure.

Workload and exam pressure were identified as the most significant sources of stress in our survey. When work is delayed, tasks accumulate closer to deadlines, increasing pressure. Planning work into smaller, achievable tasks helps reduce this build-up and gives students a greater sense of control.

Physical Activity

Physical activity can improve mood and reduce stress by supporting overall physical and mental health.

Regular physical activity supports emotional regulation and helps release tension. Even low-intensity activities, such as walking, can provide a healthy outlet for stress and improve focus when returning to academic tasks.

Reducing Stress Outflow (Coping)

Coping mechanisms are methods individuals use to respond to stress. These methods are subjective and vary between individuals.

  • Listening to music or podcasts
  • Meditation
  • Exercise
  • Journaling
  • Talking to trusted people

Our survey showed that listening to music and talking to friends were among the most common coping strategies used by students. Coping mechanisms are highly personal, meaning students often need to experiment to find methods that effectively reduce stress without causing additional harm.

Social Media and Coping

Social media can be helpful in moderation, but excessive use may become maladaptive due to its addictive nature.

According to our survey, 63% of students reported using social media as a coping mechanism. While moderate use may provide temporary distraction, excessive use can increase comparison, reduce productivity, and reinforce stress rather than relieve it.

Conclusion

While effective stress-management methods differ between individuals, combining prevention and coping strategies can significantly improve student wellbeing. Long-term stress should be addressed with external support where necessary.
